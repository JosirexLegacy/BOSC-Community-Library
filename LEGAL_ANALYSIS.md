\# Legal Analysis, Licensing the BOSC Community Library



\## Introduction



Every open-source project makes a legal decision when it chooses a license,

whether it realises it or not. Releasing work without a license doesn't make

it free it makes it legally unusable by others, because no one has been

granted permission to use, share, or build on it. This document explains the

deliberate licensing choice made for the BOSC Community Library and why it

fits the nature of this project.



\## Why MIT?



The BOSC Community Library was licensed under the \*\*MIT License\*\* for three

main reasons. First, the project's primary goal is maximum reach we want

students, educators, institutions, and developers to be able to use, share,

and build on this library without any friction. MIT is the least restrictive

widely recognised license available. Second, unlike copyleft licenses such

as GPL, MIT does not require derivative works to carry the same license,

which matters when institutions or developers want to incorporate our

resources into their own tools or platforms. Third, MIT is globally

understood and trusted a student, lecturer, or organisation in Uganda,

Kenya, or anywhere else can look at this license and know exactly what it

means without needing legal counsel.



\## Fit for a Public and Community Project



This library is not a commercial product. It is a public good maintained by

volunteer contributors. The MIT License reflects that intent clearly: it says

\*use this freely, give credit, don't hold us liable\*. That is the right

legal posture for a project that exists to serve, not to restrict.
