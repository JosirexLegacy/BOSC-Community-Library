\# Proposal for Adoption of the BOSC Community Library

\## Submitted to: The Ministry of Education, Uganda

\## Submitted by: BOSC Community (BOSC-Community-Library Project)

\## Date: May 2026



\---



\## Executive Summary



This proposal recommends that the Ministry of Education consider the

BOSC Community Library as a supplementary educational resource platform

for use in Ugandan secondary and tertiary institutions.



The BOSC Community Library is a free, open-source repository of structured

academic resources maintained by student and educator contributors. It

requires no licensing fees, no proprietary infrastructure, and no

ongoing vendor relationship. It can be deployed, adapted, and extended

by any institution without restriction.



This proposal outlines the practical case for adoption including cost

implications, accessibility benefits, and long-term sustainability.



\---



\## 1. The Problem This Addresses



Access to structured, reliable academic resources remains uneven across

Uganda's educational institutions. Students in well-resourced urban

universities have access to digital libraries, textbooks, and online

platforms. Students in regional or lower-resourced institutions often

do not.



The gap is not only financial. It is structural. Most commercial

educational platforms are designed for high-bandwidth environments,

require ongoing subscriptions, and are not adapted to local curricula

or regional contexts. They are built for markets with different needs.



An open-source, community-maintained resource library addresses this

gap directly not by substituting for existing educational infrastructure,

but by filling the spaces it does not reach.



\---



\## 2. Total Cost of Ownership



Commercial educational resource platforms typically involve:



\- Per-seat or institutional licensing fees (recurring)

\- Implementation and integration costs

\- Vendor support contracts

\- Dependency on the vendor's continued operation and pricing decisions



The BOSC Community Library involves none of these costs.



\*\*There is no licensing fee.\*\* The MIT License permits free use,

modification, and redistribution by any institution, including

government bodies, without payment or permission.



\*\*Infrastructure costs are minimal.\*\* The library is a collection of

plain markdown files hosted on GitHub. An institution that wants to

host a local mirror can do so on a basic server or even distribute

the content on USB drives for offline use. There is no database,

no application server, and no proprietary dependency.



\*\*Long-term costs do not compound.\*\* With commercial platforms,

costs typically increase over time as vendors raise prices or

change terms. With open-source, the institution owns its

deployment permanently.



A realistic total cost of ownership over five years, at institutional

scale, is close to zero in licensing terms — compared to potentially

millions of shillings per year for equivalent commercial platforms.



\---



\## 3. Avoiding Vendor Lock-In



When an institution builds its academic infrastructure on a proprietary

platform, it becomes dependent on that vendor's decisions about pricing,

availability, and direction. If the vendor raises prices, the institution

pays. If the vendor discontinues the product, the institution loses

access to years of accumulated work.



Open-source eliminates this dependency. The BOSC Community Library's

content and structure are fully owned by the community. Any institution

that adopts it retains full access to everything, permanently, regardless

of what happens to any individual contributor or hosting arrangement.



\---



\## 4. Accessibility and Offline Use



The library was designed from the beginning with low-resource environments

in mind.



\- All content is plain markdown, readable on any device, including

&#x20; low-end Android phones, without special software

\- The entire library can be downloaded as a single file (under 1MB)

&#x20; for distribution and offline use

\- Resources are written for slow or intermittent internet environments

\- A dedicated low-bandwidth study guide is included in the library



This makes it practically deployable in regional schools and universities

where broadband connectivity is unreliable or unavailable.



\---



\## 5. Local Customization



One of the most significant limitations of commercial educational platforms

is that their content is generic and internationally standardized. It is

not adapted to Ugandan curricula, local business contexts, or regional

examples.



Because the BOSC Community Library is open-source, any institution or

ministry can:



\- Add locally relevant content without permission

\- Remove or adapt content that does not fit the local curriculum

\- Create subject-specific versions for different levels of education

\- Translate materials into local languages



No commercial platform offers this flexibility. Open-source makes it

not just possible but straightforward.



\---



\## 6. Community-Driven Improvement



The library improves as more contributors participate. When students and

educators contribute resources, they are not just adding content

they are building something that belongs to their community. This

creates a fundamentally different relationship with educational material

than consuming a product built elsewhere by people with no stake in the

local context.



Ministry endorsement would accelerate this community growth significantly.

Institutions that receive official guidance to use and contribute to the

library would bring a sustained stream of knowledgeable contributors

educators who know the curriculum, students who know what's missing,

and administrators who understand the institutional context.



\---



\## 7. Educational Equity



At its core, this proposal is about equity. The same quality of structured,

accessible learning resources should be available to a student at Makerere

University and a student at a regional secondary school. Commercial models

make this difficult because they price access according to what well-resourced

institutions can pay. Open-source inverts this: the cost of access is the

same for everyone, which is zero.



The Ministry has an opportunity to formally endorse a resource that

embodies the principle that educational access should not depend on

institutional wealth.



\---



\## 8. Recommended Next Steps



Should the Ministry wish to explore adoption, the following steps are

suggested:



1\. \*\*Pilot deployment\*\* at two or three institutions, one urban, one

&#x20;  regional to assess usability and gather feedback

2\. \*\*Curriculum mapping\*\* exercise to identify gaps between current

&#x20;  library content and national curriculum requirements

3\. \*\*Contributor onboarding session\*\* with interested educators and

&#x20;  students to begin building a local contributor community

4\. \*\*Formal endorsement statement\*\* that institutions can reference

&#x20;  when recommending the library to students



None of these steps require significant budget. They require institutional

will and community engagement.



\---



\## Conclusion



The BOSC Community Library is not a finished product delivered by an

external vendor. It is a growing community resource that improves with

use and contribution. Ministry adoption would not simply give students

access to better resources — it would accelerate the development of

a locally owned, locally relevant educational commons that benefits

Ugandan students for years to come.



The library is free to use today. It is free to adapt. It is free to

build on. The only investment required is the decision to engage with it.
