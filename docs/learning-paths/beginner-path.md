\# Beginner Learning Path



This guide is for students who are starting from the beginning either new

to university, returning after a gap, or coming from a background where some

of these subjects were not well covered.



It tells you what to read, in what order, and why that order matters.

Follow it sequentially if you're unsure where to start. Skip sections you

already have a strong foundation in.



\---



\## Before You Begin



Two things first, regardless of your subject:



\*\*1. Read the study techniques guide.\*\*

Before you open a single subject resource, spend 30 minutes on this:

→ \[Study Techniques](../../resources/general/study-techniques.md)



It explains how to study in a way that actually works active recall,

spaced repetition, how to use past papers. Students who learn \*how\* to

study before diving into content retain significantly more. This is not

optional padding. It is the most valuable 30 minutes you will spend.



\*\*2. Read the academic writing guide if you have any written assessments.\*\*

→ \[Academic Writing](../../resources/general/academic-writing.md)



Most students lose marks not because they don't know the content, but

because they can't communicate it clearly. This guide fixes that.

\## Path A: Computer Science Students



Follow this sequence. Each step builds on the previous one.



\---



\### Week 1–2: Data Structures

→ \[Data Structures](../../resources/computer-science/data-structures.md)



Start here. Everything in computer science — databases, operating systems,

networking, algorithms — depends on understanding how data is stored and

organized. Don't rush this.



\*\*What to do:\*\*

\- Read the full file once for overview

\- Visit Visualgo (linked in the file) and watch each structure animate

\- Then re-read, this time taking notes on each structure

\- Test yourself: close your notes and explain each structure out loud



\*\*You're ready to move on when:\*\* you can explain the difference between

an array, a linked list, a stack, and a queue without looking at your notes.



\---



\### Week 3–4: Networking Fundamentals

→ \[Networking Fundamentals](../../resources/computer-science/networking-fundamentals.md)



Networks are how computers communicate. Understanding this makes you a

better developer and helps you debug problems that confuse most students.



\*\*What to do:\*\*

\- Read the OSI model section twice, it underpins everything else

\- Focus on TCP vs UDP and HTTP/HTTPS, these come up constantly

\- If you have a terminal available, try the practical commands listed



\*\*You're ready to move on when:\*\* you can explain what happens from the

moment you type a URL into a browser to the moment the page loads.



\---



\### Week 5–6: Operating Systems

→ \[Operating Systems](../../resources/computer-science/operating-systems.md)



The most conceptual of the three. Best approached after you understand

data structures (memory management builds on this) and networking (the OS

manages network interfaces too).



\*\*What to do:\*\*

\- Download the free OS textbook linked in the file for offline reading

\- Focus on processes, memory management, and scheduling first

\- Deadlocks are an exam favourite make sure you understand them



\*\*You're ready to move on when:\*\* you can describe what happens when you

open an application — from clicking the icon to seeing the window at the

OS level.

\## Path B: Business and Commerce Students



\---



\### Week 1: Financial Literacy

→ \[Financial Literacy](../../resources/business/financial-literacy.md)



Start here. Understanding money is foundational to every other business topic.

Entrepreneurship, marketing, operations all of it eventually comes back to

financial decisions.



\*\*What to do:\*\*

\- Read the full file

\- After reading the budgeting section, write out your own current budget

&#x20; (this is the fastest way to make the content real and memorable)

\- Make sure you understand the difference between revenue and profit before

&#x20; moving on



\---



\### Week 2–3: Entrepreneurship Basics

→ \[Entrepreneurship Basics](../../resources/business/entrepreneurship-basics.md)



\*\*What to do:\*\*

\- Read the problem-solution fit section and think of a real problem you've

&#x20; experienced in your community or university

\- Write one sentence that describes a business model that could address it

\- This is not an assignment it's a way of making the concepts concrete

\## Tracking Your Progress



You don't need any app or tool for this. A simple notebook works fine:



\- Write the resource name

\- Write the date you completed it

\- Write three things you learned from it

\- Write one question you still have



Reviewing these notes before an exam is more effective than re-reading the

original resource.
