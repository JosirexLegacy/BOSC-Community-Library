\# Legal Analysis, Licensing the BOSC Community Library



\## Introduction



Choosing a license for an open-source project is not a formality.

It is a statement about who the project is for, how it can be used,

and what kind of community it is trying to build. For the BOSC Community

Library, the licensing decision was made deliberately and with the project's

core mission in mind: removing barriers to educational access, not creating

new ones.



This document explains why the MIT License was chosen, how it fits the

nature of this project, and what it means in practice for contributors,

users, and institutions who might want to adopt or build on this work.



\---



\## Why MIT?



The MIT License is one of the oldest and most widely used open-source

licenses in existence. Its core terms are simple: anyone can use, copy,

modify, distribute, and build upon this work including for commercial

purposes, provided they include the original copyright notice. That is

essentially the entire license.



That simplicity is not a weakness. It is the point.



For a project like this one, the goal is adoption. Every additional

restriction in a license is a friction point that makes adoption slower

and more complicated. A university in Kenya, a government education

department in Uganda, a nonprofit building a learning platform for

rural students none of these should need to consult a lawyer before

using this library. With MIT, they don't have to.



\---



\## Fit for Educational and Community Projects



The MIT License is particularly well-suited to educational resource

projects for several reasons.



First, it treats users as trusted adults. It does not attempt to control

how the material is used once it leaves this repository which is the

correct posture for a project that exists to spread knowledge, not guard it.



Second, it allows institutions to integrate this library's content into

their own systems, portals, or printed materials without triggering

license compatibility issues. A school that wants to print these resources

and distribute them offline, or a developer who wants to build a study app

on top of this content, can do so immediately and legally.



Third, MIT is universally understood. Contributors from different countries

and legal backgrounds can read and understand it without ambiguity. This

matters in a community that intends to include contributors from across

East Africa and beyond, where familiarity with different license types

varies significantly.



\---



\## Comparison with GPL and Apache 2.0



Two licenses that were considered and ultimately not chosen were the

GNU General Public License (GPL) and the Apache License 2.0.



The GPL is a copyleft license: anyone who builds on GPL-licensed work

must release their derivative work under the GPL as well. This is

philosophically appealing it ensures the commons stays open. But for

an educational content library, it creates real problems. An institution

that incorporates this content into a closed internal learning system

would technically be in violation. A developer building a commercial

study app who includes adapted versions of these resources would need

to open-source their entire product. These restrictions would reduce

adoption among exactly the institutions this library is trying to serve.

The GPL protects openness at the cost of accessibility. For this project,

that trade-off is the wrong way around.



The Apache License 2.0 is also permissive, like MIT, but it is longer

and includes explicit terms around patent rights. For a software library

or codebase, those patent provisions are meaningful. For a repository of

educational markdown documents, they are unnecessary overhead. Apache 2.0

would not harm this project, but it adds complexity without adding benefit.

MIT achieves everything Apache 2.0 would for this use case, in a fraction

of the words.



\---



\## Commercial Reuse Implications



Under MIT, anyone can take this content and use it commercially. A company

could build a paid tutoring platform using resources from this library.

A publisher could incorporate adapted versions into a paid textbook.



This is intentional.



The alternative — restricting commercial use — sounds protective but

in practice reduces the reach of the work. If a well-resourced company

builds a commercial product on top of this library and reaches ten thousand

students who would otherwise not have found these resources, that is a

good outcome. The MIT License makes that possible. The only requirement

is that the original copyright notice is retained, which ensures the

BOSC Community's contribution remains visible and credited.



What MIT does not allow is someone removing the copyright notice and

claiming original authorship. That boundary is sufficient.



\---



\## Openness and Accessibility as Core Values



The choice of MIT License is ultimately an expression of what this project

values. Open-source is not just a development model it is a commitment

to the idea that knowledge belongs to everyone who needs it. In educational

contexts, especially in regions where access to quality materials is unequal,

that commitment is not abstract. It is the difference between a student

having a useful resource and not having one.



A license that restricts use, even with good intentions, contradicts that

commitment. MIT ensures that the work this community produces can travel

as far and reach as many people as possible, with the minimum possible

friction between the work and the people who need it.
